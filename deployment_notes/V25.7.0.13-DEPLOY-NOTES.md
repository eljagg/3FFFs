## Pedagogical insight (OBS-027)

**Vishing works on sophisticated victims because the script is
calibrated to their sophistication.** The educated, skeptical
business owner is more vulnerable to a well-crafted authority call
than the elderly retiree, because the script anticipates skepticism
and addresses it ("we know you're careful, that's why we're calling
YOU first"). The trainee who thinks "I'd never fall for that" is the
trainee who needs this animation most. Bank staff who recognize the
vishing kill chain in customer dispute reports recover faster than
those who treat it as user error.

## Four-lens block (OBS-018)

- **Teller:** When Devon walks in 3 days after the call to ask about
  the J$5.8M, the teller is the first responder. The animation teaches
  the recognition pattern — Devon's description of "Officer Reid from
  FID," the protective-transfer pretext, the pre-conditioned callback
  verification — so the teller can categorize this as vishing-victim-
  scenario rather than miscoding it as authorized-transfer-dispute.
  The categorization gates the recovery process.
- **Analyst:** The AML analyst sees the J$5.8M transfer to a brand-new
  beneficiary on a 4-year clean-history account, with callback
  verification ostensibly passed. Behavioral anomaly monitoring would
  surface this in real-time IF configured to weight beneficiary-novelty
  AND amount-vs-baseline-deviation, regardless of callback outcome.
  The animation shows the gap.
- **SOC:** The SOC owns callback verification protocols. This animation
  surfaces a critical lesson: callback verification can be weaponized
  if the fraudster scripts the customer to expect it. Stronger callback
  questions (asking what specifically the customer was told to expect,
  or about transfer purpose) defeat scripted authorization. Callback
  protocols need anti-coaching design.
- **Executive:** Stage 7 surfaces the pattern of "callback verification
  passed" being insufficient when scripts include callback-conditioning.
  Drives policy conversations about callback question design and
  whether passing callback automatically clears velocity holds, or
  whether layered controls (callback + behavioral anomaly score +
  beneficiary-history check) gate clearance.

### The 5 detection controls (4 real + 1 naive)

1. **Customer callback protocol** — "Hang up and call us back on the
   number on the back of your card." Removes caller-ID trust from the
   customer's decision. Catches stages 2-3 if customer recognizes the
   cue.
2. **Never-share-OTP customer training** — Catches the OTP-disclosure
   moment if Devon recognizes the SMS warning ("NEVER share this code")
   before the fraudster reframes it.
3. **Out-of-band verification for high-risk transfers** — JNCB places
   automated callback on J$5.8M transfer to new beneficiary. Catches at
   stage 7 IF the callback agent asks anti-coaching questions
   (about transfer purpose, what customer was told). The animation
   shows the gap when the callback simply asks "did you authorize?"
4. **Behavioral anomaly monitoring on business accounts** — J$5.8M to
   never-before-seen beneficiary on 4-year clean account. Hard outlier
   detectable independent of callback outcome.
5. **NAIVE: Customers should know not to talk to scammers** — Surfaces
   the institutional posture that vishing victims are "naive."
   Custom callout: "Vishing scripts are calibrated to sophisticated
   victims; the educated business owner is the target profile, not
   the exception."

### The 5 hidden signals

1. Spoofed caller-ID claiming FID — revealed by callback protocol
2. Isolation language ("do not hang up, do not call your bank") — revealed by callback protocol
3. Caller requesting OTP read aloud — revealed by OTP-disclosure training
4. Customer pre-conditioned to expect callback — revealed by out-of-band verification control (showing how it gets weaponized)
5. J$5.8M to brand-new beneficiary breaks 4-year baseline — revealed by behavioral anomaly monitoring

## Honest accounting

Three notes:

1. **The institution being impersonated is the FID** — the real
   Jamaican Financial Investigations Division. Using a real
   institution's name in the animation may feel pointed; it is
   intentional. Vishing scripts in the field name FID, JCF, and bank
   fraud-ops directly because those are the real authorities customers
   would expect to hear from. Sanitizing this would weaken the
   recognition lesson.

2. **The script structure (authority claim → skepticism handling →
   isolation → pretext → instruction → OTP disclosure → callback
   weaponization) is the documented kill chain** from public Caribbean
   AML and fraud-investigation sources. The animation surfaces it as
   pedagogy because trainees need to recognize the pattern when
   customers describe it. If you find any single stage too operationally
   detailed, tell me which one and I'll calibrate.

3. **Callback verification getting weaponized is the most important
   single lesson in this animation.** It's the institutional control
   that's hardest to deploy correctly, and the easiest for fraudsters
   to defeat through pre-conditioning. Stage 7 is where this lands.
   If your trainees come away from this animation with one takeaway,
   make it: "callback verification questions must be designed to be
   anti-coaching."

vishing kill chain has more stages and the social-engineering
mechanics need narrative space. If the runtime feels too long,
