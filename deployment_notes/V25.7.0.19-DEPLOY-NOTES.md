## Pedagogical insight (OBS-027)

**Authorized access is the most dangerous attack surface because the
bank's own audit logs treat the insider's actions as legitimate.**

Every other Phase 1 technique leaves a forensic signature the bank
can detect: phishing emails, vishing call records, SIM-swap port
events, password reset audit entries, MFA bombing patterns. F1072
leaves audit logs that look identical to legitimate teller-assisted
operations because the insider IS legitimate — until the moment they
aren't. Devon's seven J$420K transfers from Janelle's account, each
under his own login, each within his authorized scope, look exactly
like the other 280 transactions he processes per quarter.

The only controls that catch insider abuse are organizational and
engineering: behavioral analytics on access patterns (peer-comparison
flags Devon at 4.1× branch mean for non-assigned-customer access),
segregation of duties (no single employee initiates AND approves
above a threshold), four-eyes principle on high-stakes operations
(supervisor approval for transfers above J$500K, enforced by system
not policy), and cumulative-period transfer controls (>J$1M from
one account in 30 days, not >J$1M per single transaction —
structuring defeats per-transaction ceilings).

Customer-side defense doesn't apply. Janelle never logs in online.
The customer cannot be the last line of defense for insider abuse;
the bank's internal controls have to be.

For Caribbean banking trainers, the operational signal is: when a
customer disputes transactions and the audit log shows a branch
employee initiated them on a branch terminal, do not assume "if the
bank did it, it must be authorized." Run the access-pattern analysis.
Was the assigned banker the actor, or was a different employee with
no business reason to touch this account? Cross-reference against
the branch's segregation-of-duties matrix.

## Four-lens block (OBS-018)

- **Teller:** When a customer disputes transactions and the audit
  log shows a branch employee initiated them, ASK: who was the
  assigned banker? If the initiating employee is different from the
  assigned banker, ask: what was the business justification? "Cross-
  coverage when assigned banker out" is legitimate; "I was helping
  the customer who walked in" is verifiable against branch foot
  traffic logs. Document the discrepancy in the dispute file. Do
  not close the dispute on the assumption that bank-initiated equals
  customer-authorized.

- **Analyst:** F1072 has a textbook signature in aggregate:
  per-employee customer-record-access counts deviating >3× from
  branch peer mean for non-assigned-customer accounts; structured
  transfer patterns calibrated below known approval thresholds;
  recipient accounts that resolve to next-of-kin or same-address
  matches against the initiating employee's declared records.
  Build the analytics; run it quarterly minimum, monthly preferred.
  If the bank purchased behavioral-analytics software but has not
  completed integration, escalate the integration gap as a Critical
  finding — purchased-but-not-running software does not catch
  insiders.

- **SOC:** Segregation of duties must be enforced by SYSTEM, not
  POLICY. Many Caribbean banks have policies stating "transactions
  above J$500K require supervisor approval" but branch terminal
  software allows employees to complete transactions of any amount
  under their own login. Policy without enforcement is hope.
  Cumulative-period transfer controls (>J$1M from one customer
  account in 30 days) close the structuring defeat that single-
  transaction ceilings create. Both controls are well-documented
  engineering changes, not research projects.

- **Executive:** The naive control "thorough background checks at
  hiring" is presented and explicitly countered. Devon was clean at
  hire; he is not clean now. Pre-employment screening cannot catch
  employees who become fraud risks AFTER hire (financial pressure,
  coercion, gambling problem, divorce). The control that catches
  Devon is continuous behavioral monitoring of his current actions.
  Cost calculus: behavioral-analytics software runs ~6-8 figures
  USD per year for a regional bank; insider abuse runs J$ tens of
  millions per year unrecovered. The math favors the engineering
  investment at any realistic incident rate. Caribbean banks that
  underinvest in continuous monitoring relative to pre-employment
  screening inherit the loss exposure.

### Composite case grounding

Devon Walters and Mrs. Chambers are composite characters based on:
- Jamaica J$61M SIM-swap conviction case (October 2025) where two
  telco CSRs were convicted as accomplices. The animation reframes
  the pattern with a BANK insider rather than telco insider, since
  F1072 specifically addresses authorized banking-system access.
  SC006 in caribbean-scenarios.js already covers the recruited-
  insider-by-external-crew framing; this animation provides the
  complementary standalone-insider framing.
- Bank of Jamaica 2024-2025 internet-banking fraud reporting on
  the disproportionate-value-per-incident pattern of insider
  abuse (small fraction of incident counts, large fraction of
  fraud value).

Composite framing was used (rather than naming a specific public
case by name) because (a) public Caribbean F1072 cases against
banks tend to be settled out of court or under nondisclosure, with
limited public detail, and (b) attribution to a specific named
employee adds dispute risk without pedagogical value.

### Character naming note

This animation uses "Devon Walters" — the first name "Devon" first
appeared in the F1088 Vishing animation (Devon Henry, JNCB business
customer). The reuse is intentional to model that Caribbean fraud
scenarios involve real local naming patterns. The surname
disambiguates and the role context (Personal Banker employee vs
JNCB business customer) makes the role distinction immediate. If
unique-name discipline is preferred, a future minor release can
swap to a unique first name for one of the two.
