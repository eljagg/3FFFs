## Pedagogical insight (locked v25.7.0.30 — OBS-027)

**The merchant is the entry point to the customer's bank
account.** Customers cannot audit a merchant's PCI compliance
before checking out. Issuer banks cannot audit every merchant
their customers transact at. The merchant themselves often has
no operational visibility into their own checkout flow's
runtime behaviour. The defence does not live with the customer
(no leverage), nor primarily with the issuer (only post-fraud
visibility weeks-to-months downstream of the breach). It lives
with the merchant ecosystem: tokenization at the payment
processor (so the merchant never touches the card number),
hosted payment forms (so the checkout iframe runs on the
processor's domain rather than the merchant's), and Content
Security Policy + Subresource Integrity on checkout pages
(which would have prevented the web-skimmer injection in the
IslandShop case at the script-load layer).

The diagnostic protocol for analysts triaging F1042-pattern
losses is common-point-of-compromise (CPP) clustering: when a
substantial fraction of newly-defrauded cards share a common
merchant in their transaction history within a defined window,
that merchant is the likely breach point. The institutional
question for executives is whether acquirer-mandated
tokenization should be enforced at the small-merchant tier
preventatively — before the next breach demonstrates the need.

## Scenario summary

**Mr. Damian Roberts**, age 42, owner of IslandShop — a
Bridgetown, Barbados e-commerce retailer (electronics + fashion
+ home goods) that he has run for 8 years. Republic Bank
Barbados business banking customer. Runs WooCommerce on a
self-managed VPS with a payment-integration plugin (v3.2.1)
installed 4 years ago and never updated; current release
v3.8.4 patches a known XSS-via-options vulnerability that the
adversary exploits.

**8-stage breach arc:**

1. **Reconnaissance** — adversary scans Caribbean e-commerce, identifies IslandShop as high-throughput-per-card-on-file target
2. **Initial access** — plugin XSS-via-options exploited; attacker JS staged in plugin configuration; no admin alert
3. **Skimmer live** — checkout page loads attacker-controlled JS from `islandshop-analytics.com` (registered 11 days earlier); card data exfiltrated at form-submit
4. **Capture window** — 47 days, ~1,847 cards exfiltrated, merchant operations look normal throughout
5. **Exit + package** — adversary cleanly removes skimmer; dump listed on marketplace as "Caribbean Visa premium fresh · IslandShop CPP-grade"
6. **Downstream cycle** — F1043 testing validates 612 of 1,847 cards as live (Anthony Spencer's Sagicor card among them)
7. **CPP detection** — Day 78: Visa common-point-of-compromise clustering identifies IslandShop; acquirer notification; PCI forensic review mandated
8. **Forensic close-out** — root cause confirmed; IslandShop migrates to tokenized checkout + CSP/SRI; institutional question raised about acquirer-mandated tokenization at small-merchant tier

**Three detection controls:**

- **Tokenization at payment processor** (highest leverage; defeats F1042 entirely — merchant has no card data to skim)
- **CSP + SRI on checkout pages** (catches at Stage 4 by preventing skimmer script load)
- **Card-brand CPP clustering analysis** (closes discovery loop at Stage 7-8; canonical detection mechanism for breaches the merchant never identified)

**Five detection signals** authored, tied to controls per existing convention.
