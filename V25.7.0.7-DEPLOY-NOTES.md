## Four-lens block (OBS-018)

- **Teller lens:** Same disguise-reveal mental model the teller learned
  in Positioning, applied at execution. The bank-CRM view shows three
  outgoing transactions that each individually look fine: $1,950 (under
  $2K SAR threshold), $2,400 (just under $2.5K), four $440 ATM pulls
  (each under $500 cap). Toggle controls reveal each is exactly tuned
  to dodge a specific threshold. Teller's actionable insight: "if I see
  three transfers in a row that JUST pass thresholds, that's the whole
  story, not three separate stories."
- **Analyst lens:** The five toggleable controls correspond to five
  distinct evasion techniques — sub-threshold structuring, velocity-burst,
  ATM substructuring, beneficiary-velocity, geo-rotation. Each control
  reveals the SPECIFIC transaction it was tuned against. Analyst learns
  the technique-to-detection mapping by playing with the toggles.
- **SOC lens:** The controls ARE the deployable rules. SOC sees that
  with all five active, every evasion is caught at least once. With
  none active, $13,310 walks out the door. Counterfactual mode (existing
  two_views feature) lets SOC see the cumulative effect of partial
  control sets.
- **Executive lens:** Five controls = five investments. Each off = a
  gap. Headline + summary card answers "would our current control set
  have caught this?" without requiring exec to read transaction-level
  detail.

## Pedagogical insight (OBS-027)

The disguise-reveal frame appears in two places in the kill chain —
Positioning (the disguise BEFORE the attack) and Defense Evasion (the
disguise DURING the attack). Reusing the visual pattern across these
two tactics teaches that disguise is a continuous defender concern, not
a one-off moment. The user who learns to spot the e-delivery email
change in Positioning learns to spot the structured transfer pattern
in Execution as the SAME kind of cognitive move: see the bank's view,
ask "what's underneath?"

The lever this viz exists to enable: training the user's reflex to ask
"what's underneath?" when surface-level data looks normal.

### Why two_views reuse, not a new component

I considered building a bespoke Defense Evasion component but the
disguise-reveal frame fits this content NATURALLY. The bank-CRM view
shows transactions that look fine; the threat panel reveals they're
structured against specific controls; toggleable controls bridge the
gap. That's the same pedagogical mechanic as Positioning, just at a
different phase. Reusing the component:

- Reinforces the "ask what's underneath" reflex by repeating it
- Teaches the user that disguise-reveal is a GENERAL defender skill,
  not a Positioning-specific gimmick
- Ships in days, not weeks
- Means one component to maintain, not two

The remaining tactics (Initial Access, Monetization, Resource Dev,
Execution) get bespoke viz kinds because their pedagogical moments are
genuinely different — see active issue tracker above.

### Component changes: NONE

PositioningTwoViews.jsx is unchanged. The hardcoded column labels
("What your bank sees" / "What's actually happening") are intentionally
generic and apply naturally to any fraud-defender disguise-reveal viz.
The "headline" copy in the component (e.g. "The account looks normal to
your bank") also applies — Defense Evasion transactions DO look normal
to the bank until controls are toggled.

If a future tactic genuinely needs different column labels, lift them
into config-driven props at that point. Premature optimization to do it
now.

## Honest accounting

Two real architectural calls in this release:

1. **Reuse the component, not the visual variety.** Three disguise-reveal
   viz in a row (Positioning timeline, Positioning two_views, Defense
   Evasion two_views) on adjacent tactics could feel monotonous. I
   judged the pedagogical reinforcement worth more than the visual
   variety. If user feedback contradicts that, the next two tactics
   (Initial Access, Monetization) get bespoke viz anyway, so monotony
   is bounded to two adjacent tactics.

2. **Same scenario across tactics.** SC007 Allison Brown shows up in
   Positioning, and now in Defense Evasion. This is intentional — the
   continuous narrative across tactics is what makes the framework feel
   alive. A user who's already invested in Allison's story carries that
   investment into Defense Evasion. The alternative (a new fresh
   scenario per tactic) would mean five separate cognitive setups.

If either call feels wrong on contact with reality, the fix is small:
swap in different content (config change only, no component change),
or split SC007 into per-phase variants. Both are cheap backouts.
