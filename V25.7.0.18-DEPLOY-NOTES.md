## Pedagogical insight (OBS-027)

**The bank's own authentication infrastructure is the attack vector.**

Every Phase 1 animation so far has framed the attack as something
the *attacker does to the customer through external means*: phishing
SMS, vishing call, swapped SIM, password reset. T1621 inverts the
framing: the attack is the customer being bombarded with push
notifications **from the bank's own MFA system**, weaponized via
attacker-controlled login spam.

This matters pedagogically because the trainee's intuition that "we
deployed MFA, so push-based authentication is secure" is the wrong
frame. Push-based MFA is secure ONLY IF the bank's MFA-generation
system has rate limits, account-lockout-on-N-rejections, number-
matching, and login-context display. Without these, push-MFA is a
UI for the attacker to bombard the customer into compliance.

The diagnostic signal for tellers handling disputes: when a customer
reports "I got a bunch of bank notifications I didn't ask for, and
I tapped one of them to make it stop" — the kill chain is T1621.
Audit log confirms: N push-MFA requests in a short window, mostly
denied, then one approval, then the fraudulent transaction. Cross-
reference the credential against breach corpora; find the leaked
password. The attack chain is breach corpus → credential stuffing
→ MFA bombing → fatigue approval → transfer.

## Four-lens block (OBS-018)

- **Teller:** When a customer disputes transactions and reports
  "my phone wouldn't stop buzzing with bank notifications and I
  finally tapped one" — do not anchor on phishing or vishing. This
  is T1621. Pivot intake to: "How many notifications did you get?
  Roughly when did they start? Did the buzzing only stop after you
  tapped Approve?" Document the burst pattern in the dispute file —
  it is the diagnostic signature.

- **Analyst:** T1621 leaves a textbook signature in the audit log:
  N push-MFA requests for one account in a sub-15-minute window,
  most denied or auto-expired, one approval, immediate large-
  transfer-to-new-beneficiary post-approval. The triple-signal
  (high-rate MFA + late approval + immediate transfer) should be
  a high-confidence hold-for-callback velocity rule. If the bank
  does not have this rule, propose it citing this animation.

- **SOC:** Rate-limit MFA request generation per account. Auto-lock
  account after N denials within a window (5 within 30 minutes is a
  reasonable starting point). Add login-context display to push
  prompts (location, device, time). Add number-matching MFA
  (Microsoft Authenticator pattern, 2023). All four are well-
  documented engineering changes, not research projects.

- **Executive:** The naive control "customers should know to never
  approve a push they did not initiate" is presented and explicitly
  countered. Behavioral guidance cannot solve infrastructure-level
  vulnerabilities. The fix is engineering. Cost-of-incident vs cost-
  of-engineering-change calculus favors the engineering at any
  realistic incident volume — and Caribbean banks rolling out push-
  MFA without these defenses inherited the vulnerability along with
  the convenience.

### Composite case grounding

Pat Henriques is a composite character based on the documented
T1621 technique pattern:
- Microsoft Threat Intelligence reporting on MFA fatigue (2022-2024)
- Uber September 2022 breach (the canonical published T1621 case;
  attacker bombarded a contractor with 28 MFA pushes, contractor
  approved one)
- Cisco August 2022 breach (similar pattern)
- NCB and JNCB rollout of push-MFA in 2022-2024 — the prerequisite
  Caribbean infrastructure for T1621 against bank customers
- NCB Special Investigations public reporting (Dane Nicholson 2022)
  on infostealer-malware-derived credentials feeding downstream
  account takeover — the credential-source pipeline T1621 attackers
  use

Composite framing was used (rather than naming a public Caribbean
victim by name) because (a) public Caribbean cases of T1621 against
banks are not yet documented at scale (the technique is recent and
banks under-disclose) and (b) the global technique pattern is
extremely well-documented and transfers cleanly to the Caribbean
banking environment given the deployed push-MFA infrastructure.

Three notes:

1. **Composite character framing.** Same pattern as F1018.001 —
   public Caribbean T1621 cases are not yet documented at scale
   because the technique is recent and banks under-disclose
   incidents. The global technique pattern (Microsoft Threat Intel
   + Uber + Cisco) is extensively documented and transfers cleanly
   to Caribbean banks given the push-MFA infrastructure they've
   deployed. If a public Caribbean T1621 case surfaces, future
   revision can re-anchor.

2. **The naive control framing is sharper here than in earlier
   animations.** "Customers should know to never approve unsolicited
   pushes" is a position that almost every Caribbean bank's
   customer-education material currently contains. The animation
   surfaces this directly and explains why it cannot work
   structurally — customers do legitimately receive unsolicited
   pushes (teller-assisted transactions), customers are tired and
   distracted, and behavioral training does not raise the human
   exhaustion ceiling. The four-lens block above carries the
   institutional-fix argument.

3. **No engine changes.** The animation uses MultiActorSequence
   (the same engine as Phishing/Vishing/SIM Swap/Password Reset).
   All architectural and UX fixes from v25.7.0.15.x and v25.7.0.17
   apply automatically. The 3-engine architecture continues to
   scale across Phase 1.
