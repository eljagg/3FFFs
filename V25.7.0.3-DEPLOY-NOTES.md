**Driver:** First of seven planned tactic-level visualizations covering all of F3. ## What ships

A new visualization kind `positioning_timeline`, attached to tactic FA0001 (Positioning). It renders on the Framework page when a user expands the Positioning tactic, and is **scenario-aware** — when the renderer receives a `scenarioId` prop, it picks the matching example from `config.examples`; otherwise it shows the first example as the representative case.

Two examples ship with this release, drawn from real scenarios:
- **SC007 — Account Rental** (the money mule pipeline). 30-day timeline. Three real signals: e-delivery change to throwaway Gmail (day 8), mobile device fingerprint anomaly (day 1), incoming-only transaction pattern (day 14). Four controls: auto-freeze on early e-delivery change, device velocity alert, incoming-only pattern anomaly, and one deliberately-naive "SMS the customer" control that demonstrates why naive controls fail when the mule controls the device.
- **SC008 — ATM Skimming** (Sam Sharpe Square tourist zone). 5-day timeline. Three real signals: shared-ATM fraud cluster, graph-analytics-derived victim linkage, tourist-zone fraud volume baseline spike. Four controls including two naive ones (manual CCTV review at every Mobay ATM, wait-for-customer-complaints) that demonstrate the cost-prohibitive vs. cost-effective control trade-off.

## How the visualization teaches

Three-lane horizontal timeline:
1. **Attacker** (top, terracotta): what the attacker is doing on each day
2. **Signals** (middle, accent): detection signals that appear over time
3. **Controls** (bottom, success): user-toggled defender controls, which fire on the day they would catch the attack

The user drags a scrubber from day 0 to day N (where N = totalDays). As the scrubber moves, attacker actions and signals appear at their day positions. The user toggles defender controls on/off. Each active control declares which signal IDs would trigger it; when scrubbed past that signal's day, the control "fires" on that day. The earliest fire-day across all active controls becomes the `earliestCatchDay` — the visualization's primary outcome metric.

The outcome strip at the bottom adapts to four states:
- No controls active → red, "attacker reaches execution undetected"
- Controls active but none match → red, "selected controls would not fire on this scenario"
- Controls active, would fire later → neutral, "selected controls fire on day X — scrub forward to see"
- Controls fired by current day → green, "caught on day X — N days before execution"

This makes the value of each control concrete: the user sees not just "this control catches it" but "this control catches it on day 8, this other control on day 14, picking both gets you caught earlier."

## Pedagogical choice — why a timeline

Positioning is the F3-unique tactic that ATT&CK collapses into Execution. Most fraud-ops staff coming from MITRE-shaped training don't have a strong mental model for it. The teaching insight: **positioning is fundamentally about time** — the attacker waits, behaves, and slowly establishes durable control. Static diagrams can't show the "patient phase." Only a scrubbable timeline makes "attacker had 22 days where this signal was visible" feel real.

The scrubber-with-density-ticks pattern (signal-day markers above the scrubber) lets the user see "the meaningful days" without sliding blindly. The earliest-catch indicator above the scrubber gives immediate feedback when controls activate.

## Interactivity Design (OBS-018)

### Frontline / Teller

**Material change.** Tellers are explicitly in the audience for this viz (unlike the Reconnaissance kill-chain, which excluded them in v25.7.0.2). Reason: positioning signals like "e-delivery email changed within 10 days of account opening" or "first three transactions are all incoming with no salary" are observable at the teller window. The timeline pattern reinforces "what to notice when this account walks up to my counter." The SC007 example is teller-relevant — money mules show specifically these positioning patterns. The SC008 example is more analyst-relevant but still teaches the temporal pattern that fraud-ops uses to triage.

### Fraud Analyst

**Material change.** This is the analyst's primary visualization for FA0001. The two examples cover both account-control positioning (SC007) and physical-access positioning (SC008), which are the two patterns analysts most need to recognize. The control-toggle interaction lets analysts reason about "if I had this rule active, when would it have caught it" — exactly the question they're asked when proposing new fraud rules to risk committees. The naive controls (SMS the customer, wait for complaints) demonstrate why obvious controls fail, supporting the analyst's case for graph-based controls in their next rule proposal.

### SOC / Cyber Team

**Material change.** SOC users see the same content but interpret it through detection-engineering lens — the controls list maps to specific detection rules they'd write. The "Would fire on day X" hint on each control teaches them that detection latency is itself a measurable property of a rule, not just precision/recall. The SC008 example (graph analytics linking ATM victims) is a SOC-relevant detection-engineering pattern that maps cleanly to other shared-resource fraud detection.

### Risk Manager / Exec

**Material change.** The outcome strip is the exec-relevant output. "Caught on day 8, that's 22 days before execution" is a metric an exec can understand and communicate to a board. The viz makes the "we have rules to catch this earlier vs. we wait for customer complaints" trade-off concrete and quantitative — risk managers can use this to justify investment in graph analytics or device-fingerprinting infrastructure. The naive-control patterns (cost-prohibitive CCTV review, customer-complaint reactive posture) are the controls execs sometimes default to under cost pressure; seeing them visualized as "no detection lights up" is more persuasive than a written argument.

## Honest reflections

**What I'm confident about:**
- The architecture supports the planned six remaining vizzes without further infrastructure changes. Each new viz adds: one component file, one switch case in VisualizationRenderer, one entry in the visualizations seed. The scenario-awareness pattern (forward `scenarioId`, fall back to first example) generalizes cleanly.
- The two examples are scenario-grounded — every signal in the timeline traces back to a real signal in the SC007-S2 or SC008-S2 stage seed. An auditor reviewing the viz can verify it isn't fabricating fraud-ops claims.
- The outcome-strip framing ("caught on day X, N days before execution") is concrete enough for exec consumption and specific enough for analyst engineering decisions.

**What's deliberately not in this release:**
- Scenario-page integration. The viz is rendered only on the Framework page when FA0001 is expanded. Wiring it into Scenario.jsx so a user inside SC007 sees the SC007 timeline below their stage progression is a separate decision (touches Scenario.jsx layout, which has been heavily modified recently).
- Per-bank customization of the timeline content. A future v25.7.x could let a bank set `bankSpecificControls` that swap in their actual deployed rule names, but for v25.7.0.3 the controls are scenario-generic.
- Mobile rendering optimization. The timeline assumes desktop or tablet width; on a sub-400px viewport the scrubber and lanes will compress awkwardly. Acceptable for v25.7.0.3 since the primary audience is bank staff at workstations.

**What's worth watching:**
- Animation performance with many signal markers. The current examples have 3 signals each; if a future scenario configures 10+ signals, the absolute-positioned markers may overlap visually. Easy fix later (cluster overlapping markers) but not blocking.
- The "earliestCatchDay" calculation runs on every render of the parent component. With more controls/signals it could become a perf concern, but at current scale (4 controls × 3 signals) it's negligible. Already memoized.
