## Pedagogical insight (OBS-027)

**Account takeover via password reset is the silent attack vector.**

Phishing (F1081) requires the customer to click. Vishing (F1088)
requires the customer to answer. SIM Card Swap (T1451) requires
telco insider involvement and produces a "no service" symptom on
the victim's phone. F1018.001 requires none of these. The victim
is never contacted. There is no link to recognize, no caller to
verify, no service interruption to investigate. The attacker walks
through the bank's own self-service password-reset flow using OSINT-
gathered answers to security questions, takes over the account, and
moves funds. The customer discovers the loss only when checking
their balance.

For Caribbean banking trainers, the operational signal that points
to F1018.001 specifically is **the absence of customer-side
artifacts** in the dispute. When a customer says "I never logged
in, I never got an OTP, I never got a call from the bank, I have
no missing SMS notifications" — that is not a customer who is
mis-remembering. That is the signature of F1018.001. The kill chain
left no breadcrumbs in the customer's experience because the
customer was never part of the transaction.

The dispute-handling skill that flows from this is: do not anchor
on phishing/vishing/SIM-swap kill chains when none of the customer-
side signals are present. Pull the audit log; check for a recent
password reset; cross-reference the security-question answers
against the customer's public profile. The match is usually
obvious.

## Four-lens block (OBS-018)

- **Teller:** When a customer disputes transactions and the standard
  phishing/vishing intake interview produces no customer-side signals
  ("I never clicked anything, I never got any call"), do not assume
  the customer is hiding something or mis-remembering. Pivot the
  intake to F1018.001 questions: "When did you last reset your
  password? Do you have any unread emails from the bank? Has anyone
  asked you about your mother's maiden name or where you went to
  school recently?" Document the absence-of-artifacts pattern in the
  dispute file — it is the diagnostic signature.

- **Analyst:** F1018.001 disputes have a distinct fraud-pattern
  signature in the audit log: password reset event timestamp,
  followed within minutes by login from a new device/IP, followed
  within minutes by add-beneficiary + large transfer. The triple-
  signal (recent reset + new device + new beneficiary) should be a
  high-confidence hold-for-callback velocity rule. If the bank does
  not have this rule, propose it citing this animation.

- **SOC:** The reset-flow itself is the institutional vulnerability.
  Knowledge-based authentication on high-stakes account changes is
  structurally broken in 2026 because answer privacy is no longer
  achievable via OSINT. Retire knowledge-based auth from the reset
  flow; require MFA via SMS-to-registered-phone or push-to-
  registered-device. This is a product/policy change, not a
  monitoring change.

- **Executive:** The naive control "customers should choose stronger
  security questions" is presented and explicitly countered in the
  animation. Customer-choice solutions cannot fix institutional
  authentication design. The fix is to retire knowledge-based auth
  on the reset flow — a product decision the bank can make
  unilaterally without customer training cost. Cost-of-incident vs
  cost-of-product-change calculus favors the product change at any
  realistic incident volume.

### Composite case grounding

Andre Lewis is a composite character based on documented Caribbean
banking ATO patterns:
- NCB Special Investigations public reporting (Dane Nicholson, 2022) on password-reset-themed phishing → ATO
- Jamaica Bankers Association anti-fraud committee classification of password-reset ATO as a top-three channel
- Shelly-ann Watt Gammon NCB case (Jamaica Observer, 2023) — silent compromise pattern matches the F1018.001 signature

Composite framing was used (rather than naming a public victim by
name) because (a) the documented public cases focus on phishing-then-
OTP rather than pure F1018.001, and (b) attribution to a specific
victim adds dispute risk without pedagogical value. The case is
grounded in real patterns; the character is invented to carry the
pattern.

## Honest accounting

Three notes:

1. **Composite character vs named-case grounding.** Phishing (F1081),
   Vishing (F1088), and SIM Swap (T1451) all named real public
   victims (Beverly composite, Devon Henry pattern, Tanya Ricketts
   conviction case). F1018.001 used a composite character because
   the documented public cases of pure F1018.001 in Caribbean
   banking are limited — the most cited cases mix F1018.001 with
   phishing-then-OTP. Composite framing keeps the pedagogy clean
   for the technique. If a documented pure-F1018.001 case surfaces,
   future revision can re-anchor.

2. **The naive control framing is sharper here than in earlier
   animations.** "Customers should choose stronger security
   questions" is a position that almost every Caribbean bank's
   customer-education material currently contains. The animation
   surfaces this directly and explains structurally why it doesn't
   work. The four-lens block above carries the institutional-fix
   argument. This is intentional — the animation should change the
   conversation about who is responsible for fixing F1018.001.

3. **No engine changes.** The animation uses MultiActorSequence (the
   same engine as Phishing/Vishing/SIM Swap). All architectural fixes
   from v25.7.0.15.x apply. No new engine work was needed; the
   3-engine architecture continues to scale across Phase 1.
