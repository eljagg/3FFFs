## Pedagogical insight (OBS-027)

**MFA is only as secure as the channel that delivers the second
factor.**

T1111 is the Phase 1 Initial Access technique that completes the
MFA-bypass family alongside F1018.001 (bypasses MFA via reset
flow) and T1621 (defeats MFA via fatigue). T1111 is structurally
different from both: the bank deployed MFA correctly, the customer
received the second-factor token correctly, and the attacker still
got in — because the delivery channel (SMS) is structurally
compromised in a world of mobile banking trojans.

The diagnostic signature for fraud analysts: customer reports
unauthorized transactions; audit log shows a CORRECT OTP entered
at login; customer confirms they received the OTP SMS but did NOT
initiate the login. The OTP was correct because it was the bank's
real OTP — the attacker received it via the customer's compromised
phone. This is structurally different from phishing (where the
customer entered the OTP into a fake page) and SIM swap (where
the SIM itself was compromised at the telco level). The phone is
the customer's phone, the SIM is the customer's SIM, but a
malicious app installed on the phone reads the OTP SMS and
forwards it to the attacker before — or shortly after — the
customer reads it.

The institutional fix is not customer education about app
installation hygiene. That has been demonstrated empirically not
to scale, and even Google Play has shipped banking trojans
(documented Joker family 2023, Anatsa family 2024). The
institutional fix is to retire SMS as the second-factor delivery
channel for high-stakes account access:
- Push-based MFA via the bank's own app for retail customers
- App-based authenticator codes (TOTP) as a smartphone-friendly
  alternative
- FIDO2 hardware tokens for high-value business accounts
- Voice OTP via callback as a fallback for customers without
  smartphones (the malware reads SMS, not call audio)

## Four-lens block (OBS-018)

- **Teller:** When a customer reports unauthorized transactions
  AND the audit log shows the OTP was entered correctly, do NOT
  default to "the customer must have entered the OTP somewhere
  they shouldn't have." T1111 produces correct-OTP-via-malware
  with no customer interaction at all. Pivot intake to: "Did
  you receive the OTP SMS? Did you read it? Did you enter it
  anywhere yourself?" If the customer received the SMS but
  did not enter it anywhere, this is T1111. Document the
  pattern.

- **Analyst:** T1111 has a textbook signature in the audit log:
  correct password (often from breach corpus) + correct OTP +
  impossible-travel anomaly score on the login + rapid transfer
  to new beneficiaries. Cross-reference the customer's
  credentials against breach corpora to confirm the credential-
  stuffing entry path. Forensic examination of the customer's
  phone confirms malware presence — a known banking trojan
  family signature in the package list.

- **SOC:** Three controls reduce T1111 exposure: (a) retire SMS
  as MFA channel for high-stakes auth; (b) hard-block (not
  flag-only) impossible-travel logins; (c) integrate mobile
  malware detection into the bank's own app. Of the three, (a)
  is the institutional fix that defeats the technique entirely.
  (b) and (c) are mitigations. Caribbean banks running SMS
  OTP as the default second factor inherit the structural
  exposure regardless of how many anomaly-detection systems are
  layered on top.

- **Executive:** The naive control "customers should only
  install apps from official app stores" is presented and
  explicitly countered. Customer-vigilance solutions cannot fix
  the structural channel vulnerability. Even when customers
  follow the advice perfectly, banking trojans ship through
  Google Play. The fix is engineering — retire SMS as the MFA
  channel. Cost calculus: push-MFA infrastructure runs ~6-7
  figures USD per year for a regional bank's full rollout;
  T1111 incidents in Caribbean banking are documented in BOJ
  reports as a growing fraction of internet-banking fraud value.
  The math favors the engineering investment at any realistic
  incident rate.
