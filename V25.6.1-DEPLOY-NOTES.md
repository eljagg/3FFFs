# v25.6.1 — CBEST FrameworkPhase nodes + FrameworkPhaseSidebar

**Release type:** Schema addition + new UI wedge (the third alongside Concept and MITRE)
**Driver:** ISS-013 — CBEST FrameworkPhase taxonomy not yet modelled
**Source of truth:** Bank of England, *CBEST Threat Intelligence-Led Assessments — Implementation Guide*, 2024 edition (PRA, CC-BY 4.0). Specifically Figures 3 + 4 (phases and timeline) and §6 / §7 / §8 / §9 (per-phase narrative).

This is the release where OBS-018 (four-level Interactivity Design) starts genuinely paying off. Every CBEST FrameworkPhase node carries explicit role-conditional content for all four user levels (teller, analyst, soc, executive), and the FrameworkPhaseSidebar lets the user switch lens via tabs. The schema is the means; the four-level content is the end.

---

## Summary

**20 new FrameworkPhase nodes** for CBEST: 5 top-level phases (Initiation, Threat Intelligence, Penetration Testing, Closure, Post-CBEST Thematic) + 15 sub-stages (matching BoE Figure 3 exactly).

**3 new edge types:**
- `HAS_PHASE` (CBEST Framework → top-level phases)
- `HAS_SUBPHASE` (top-level phases → sub-stages)
- `IN_FRAMEWORK_PHASE` (SC013/SC014 stages → specific sub-stages)

**Stage→phase mapping:** 9 SC013 stages mapped to Phases 1.2 / 1.3 / 1.4 / 2.3 / 3.2 / 4.1; all 8 SC014 stages mapped to Phase 3.2 (Execution — they all play during the live testing window).

**New API endpoint** `/api/framework-phases/:id` and `/api/framework-phases/:id/practiced-in` (mirrors MITRE pattern, public reference content).

**New client component** `FrameworkPhaseSidebar.jsx` — the third wedge alongside ConceptSidebar (v25.4.2) and MitreTechniqueSidebar (v25.5.1). Built around four-lens role tabs.

**New chip on stages** — CBEST-orange dotted-border chip reading `CBEST Phase 1.3` (or whatever sub-stage applies). Visually distinct from concept (solid accent border) and MITRE (dashed ink-soft border).

## Schema design — Option A flat hierarchy

Decision recorded for traceability: 19 nodes total — 5 top-level phases + 15 sub-stages — modelled as a single `:FrameworkPhase` label with `level: 'phase' | 'sub-stage'` and `parentId` pointing to the parent (null for top-level). Plus parent→child `HAS_SUBPHASE` edges to make hierarchy queries trivial without committing to a new label. The flat pattern matches AASE phases today and lets us upgrade to a nested `:FrameworkSubPhase` label later if needed without breaking existing queries.

## Schema design — Option X chip granularity

Decision recorded: chip shows the **sub-stage**, not the parent phase. Click `CBEST Phase 1.3` → sidebar opens to *Phase 1.3 Scoping*, which shows the parent phase context. Matches the SC013 narrative which already references sub-stage numbers ("Phase 1.3 of CBEST is Scoping...").

## Interactivity Design (OBS-018)

This is v25.6.1's payoff section — read it carefully. The four-level interactivity is not just "the sidebar shows different text per role" — it's that a real frontline teller, a real fraud analyst, a real SOC engineer, and a real exec each get content calibrated to their lens, their stake, and their decision space. Same phase, four genuinely different perspectives.

### Frontline / Teller

**What changes:** SC014 (the helpdesk-vishing scenario) gets a CBEST chip on every stage, all linking to Phase 3.2 (Execution). Tellers who land on SC014 — possibly via curiosity, possibly via a manager's recommendation — can now see that the helpdesk-vishing TTP they're learning to spot is the SAME live-test technique a CBEST PT provider would use. The framework anchor connects "this lesson" to "this is what real attacks against UK retail banks look like."

**What they see:** When a teller-role user opens any SC014 stage, the CBEST chip reads "Phase 3.2 Execution". Tap → sidebar opens with the **Frontline lens active by default** (because their role is teller). Content for Frontline / Teller on Phase 3.2:

> "The 14-week window where the test is actually happening. Continue normal operations exactly as you would on any other day."
>
> - Helpdesk vishing, phishing emails, suspicious supplier requests — your normal vigilance is the test.
> - Report anything suspicious through normal channels. Do not investigate yourself.

The teller can switch to the SOC lens or Executive lens via tabs to see how the same phase looks from another seat — useful for context but not what they need to act on.

**What they learn:** The training they do every week (phish recognition, vishing scripts, supplier verification) is graded by an external testing firm during these 14-week windows. Their everyday vigilance is the actual subject of the test. Builds motivation by connecting lesson → real-world stakes.

### Fraud Analyst

**What changes:** Every SC013 stage gets a CBEST chip linked to its specific sub-stage (1.2 → 4.1). The analyst can now read SC013 as a guided tour of the entire CBEST procedural arc, not just a sequence of decision points. The chip becomes the navigation device — tap any stage's CBEST chip to anchor it in the framework.

**What they see:** Analyst lens is the default for users with the analyst role. Content is technical/procedural with deliverable references. For Phase 1.3 Scoping (SC013-S2):

> "IBS list, supporting key systems, and compromise actions all written down and signed off. Firm-led with regulator review — the firm proposes; the regulator validates against threat intelligence. This is SC013-S2."
>
> - Three layers in the Scope Specification: (i) IBSs, (ii) systems that underpin each, (iii) compromise actions targeted.
> - Compromise actions: confidentiality, integrity, availability — pick those relevant to each IBS.
> - The CG proposes; deferring entirely to the regulator inverts the design and reads as a finding.
> - Practiced in SC013-S2.

Sub-stages section shows Phase 1's other three sub-stages (1.1, 1.2, 1.4) for context. Practiced In section shows SC013-S2.

**What they learn:** The vocabulary they would use in a real CBEST conversation, the deliverable taxonomy (CBEST 2024d for Scope Specification, 2024e for Targeting Report, etc.), and the procedural rhythm. By the time they've walked SC013, they've effectively walked the BoE Implementation Guide — every section signposted by a chip.

### SOC / Cyber Team

**What changes:** SC014 — entirely a SOC-tier scenario — gets the framework anchor that explains *why* the hard-mode decisions in S2/S3/S4/S6 matter. The CBEST chip on every SC014 stage links to Phase 3.2 (Execution), which is the phase where SOC capability is being graded.

**What they see:** SOC lens for Phase 3.2:

> "This is the phase that puts you in the hot seat. For 14 weeks, attacks based on the Phase 2 scenarios will be hitting your monitored systems. You will not know which incidents are tests and which are real. Your decision-making during this phase is what the D&R Capability Assessment grades."
>
> - Live production. The attacks are real techniques against real systems.
> - Test/Halt model: if you escalate the wrong way, the test is degraded; if you escalate poorly, it is a finding.
> - Some signals will be testers; some will be real attackers. Treat everything as real until proven otherwise.
> - Practiced in all of SC014.

Crucially, the SOC user can also switch to the **Executive lens** to see the *governance* framing of the same phase ("CG retains halt authority...") — which calibrates what their escalation actually triggers upstream.

**What they learn:** Why SC014's "treat everything as real" rule isn't just good hygiene — it's the explicit assumption baked into the CBEST Test/Halt model. The training reinforces a real procedural discipline, not a pedagogic preference.

### Risk Manager / Exec

**What changes:** Executives get the **governance and accountability** lens on every CBEST sub-stage. Where the analyst sees "IBS triage decision" the executive sees "Scope Specification sign-off — one of three executive-decision moments in CBEST." The CBEST framework arc is now navigable as a sequence of three accountability moments (scope sign-off, procurement gate, remediation prioritisation), each linked to a specific SC013 stage and a specific BoE section.

**What they see:** Executive lens for Phase 1.3 Scoping:

> "You sign off the Scope Specification. This is one of three executive-decision moments in the CBEST cycle (the others being procurement and remediation prioritisation). The IBS selection becomes the entire downstream test."
>
> - Scope Specification (CBEST 2024d) signed off by you; the regulator countersigns.
> - Scope must be representative of the firm's real IBSs — over-narrowing the scope to dodge findings is itself a finding.
> - Third-party / supplier-underpinned IBSs in scope require Supplementary Guidance (CBEST 2024k) handling.
> - Practiced in SC013-S2 — the IBS triage decision.

**What they learn:** When (and where, and why) executive sign-off matters in a CBEST. Procurement gate → SC013-S3-CONSEQUENCE shows the real cost of misapplying it (4–6 week slippage + supervisory note). Remediation prioritisation → SC013-S6 shows the supervisory consequence of getting it wrong. The framework anchor turns abstract governance into specific, traceable decisions.

### Cross-lens behaviour (the genuinely interactive bit)

The sidebar's lens tabs aren't gated — any user can read any lens. That's deliberate. The most useful interactive moment is when a fraud analyst on SC013-S6 (remediation prioritisation) clicks the **Executive lens** to see how the *same* decision reads from upstairs. Or when an executive on SC013-S2 (scoping) clicks the **SOC lens** to see what the secrecy obligation actually means for their security operations team. The chip and sidebar make perspective-switching one tap away — that's the whole point of the four-level structure.

A small accent dot next to the user's own role tab tells them which lens is "theirs" so they don't lose the thread when exploring.

## Honest reflections on tradeoffs

**The Concept ID rename is still deferred.** v25.6.0 renamed the display name to "CBEST Accreditation" but kept the graph ID `CONCEPT-CREST-ACCREDITATION`. The same convention applies here: graph IDs are stable, display values are flexible. v25.6.1 doesn't touch this; same v25.6.x cleanup target.

**AASE FrameworkPhase enrichment deferred.** AASE has 4 phase nodes already in the graph but no `roleContent` and no chip wedge. The component is framework-agnostic — when AASE phases get the same roleContent shape, they automatically get the wedge. v25.6.x can backport AASE without further client changes.

**Quizzes scoped to FrameworkPhase nodes deferred.** Could have been bundled but expands the test surface. v25.6.4 candidate.

**Cross-jurisdictional CBEST framing deferred.** BoE doc explicitly mentions cross-jurisdictional CBESTs (FMI participants outside the UK). Worth a future scenario but not v25.6.1's job.

**Risk of regression:** All edges added are new types (HAS_PHASE, HAS_SUBPHASE, IN_FRAMEWORK_PHASE). No existing edges deleted. SC001–SC012 see zero change to their stage payloads (frameworkPhase is null). The migration is idempotent-aware from line one (canonical pattern, OBS-019-clean from start).
