## Pedagogical insight (OBS-027)

**SIM swap fraud is not primarily a customer-vigilance failure or a
technology failure. In the Caribbean pattern documented by the J$61M
Jamaica conviction (October 2025), the failure is the insider** —
telco customer service representatives accepting payment to port
victim numbers. The customer cannot see the port happening; the bank
cannot see the SIM change at the carrier; the carrier's own controls
are defeated by employees with legitimate access.

Defense requires institution-side controls that operate ACROSS the
bank-telco boundary: SIM-swap-API risk scoring (when did this number
last port?), behavioral anomaly monitoring on logins from new devices
immediately after a SIM change, and customer training that recognizes
the "no service" signal as a fraud cue, not a phone problem.

**Bank staff who recognize the SIM-swap kill chain in customer dispute
reports recover faster than those who treat it as user error.** Tanya
did nothing wrong — strong password, careful with phishing, did not
share OTP. Her vulnerability was structural to SMS-2FA + insider
co-conspirators.

## Four-lens block (OBS-018)

- **Teller:** When Tanya walks in the next morning to dispute the
  J$1.2M transfer, the teller is the first responder. The animation
  teaches the recognition pattern — "my phone went NO SERVICE around
  2:34 PM yesterday, then I learned my number had been ported" — so
  the teller can categorize this as SIM-swap-victim-scenario rather
  than miscoding it as authorized-transfer-dispute or
  password-compromise. The categorization gates the recovery process
  AND escalates to fraud-ops with the right context (Digicel
  porting records need to be requested; the cascade across other
  accounts needs to be assessed).
- **Analyst:** The AML analyst sees a transfer initiated from a
  never-seen device on Tanya's account, password reset 4 minutes
  before login, OTP authorization passing. Behavioral anomaly
  monitoring catches "new device + new beneficiary + amount-near-
  balance" if configured to weight all three. SIM-swap-API integration
  catches the recent-port event independent of any other signal.
- **SOC:** The SOC owns the bank-telco SIM-swap-API integration
  decision. Currently most Caribbean banks do not query carrier
  SIM-swap APIs. This is a deployable control with public GSMA
  standards (Telefónica Open Gateway and similar). The animation
  surfaces what's possible and what's missed when it isn't deployed.
- **Executive:** Stage 8 surfaces that SMS-2FA is a single point of
  failure across the customer's entire digital identity — the cascade
  shown in stage 7 (Scotia → Gmail → Apple ID → Sagicor pension) is
  the structural truth. Migration to authenticator apps or FIDO2
  passkeys is an executive-level decision about the bank's
  authentication architecture. Drives policy conversations about
  whether SMS-2FA is acceptable for high-value transfers.

### The 5 detection controls (4 real + 1 naive)

1. **Bank-side SIM-swap API risk scoring** — Catches stages 5-6.
   GSMA-standard SIM-swap API (Telefónica Open Gateway, similar)
   lets banks query "when did this customer's number last port?"
   If the answer is "47 minutes ago," every authentication signal
   from that number is downgraded for a 24-72h window.
2. **Telco insider-action monitoring** — Catches stage 3. Carrier-side
   monitoring on CSR port volumes per shift, port destinations, and
   ports executed without standard authentication. The 23-ports-in-9-
   days pattern would surface in real-time. The five-year delay
   before the J$61M case surfaced reflects this monitoring not being
   deployed.
3. **Customer "no service" training as fraud cue** — Catches stage 4.
   Trains customers: NO SERVICE while others on the same network
   have signal = fraud cue. Borrow another phone immediately, call
   carrier + bank fraud lines. The 10-minute window before the first
   fraudulent transfer is the recovery window.
4. **New-device login + immediate transfer velocity hold** — Catches
   stage 6. Bank holds high-value transfer initiated from device-
   fingerprint never seen on the account, within minutes of initial
   login from that device. Tanya's account has 8 years of device
   history; the new device + new beneficiary + amount-near-balance
   triple-anomaly should trigger a callback hold.
5. **NAIVE: Customers should use stronger passwords** — Surfaces
   the failed institutional posture that SIM swap is a credential-
   hygiene problem. Password strength does not protect a number that
   ports. Custom callout names the actual attack surface: insider
   co-conspirators at the carrier, not customer password strength.

### The 5 hidden signals

1. Number ported in the last hour — revealed by SIM-swap API control
2. CSR ported 23 numbers in 9 days, all to nearby IMEI cluster — revealed by insider monitoring
3. Tanya's phone shows NO SERVICE while others have signal — revealed by no-service training
4. Login + transfer from never-seen device — revealed by new-device velocity control
5. Single port cascades to 4 separate accounts — revealed by SIM-swap API control (the structural lesson)

## Honest accounting

Three notes:

1. **The animation surfaces telco insider involvement directly.** This
   is documented public record from the J$61M conviction — two of
   three convicted were Digicel customer service representatives.
   The lesson lands harder when the structural failure is concrete.
   If the framing feels uncomfortable to telco staff in your training
   audience, that discomfort is pedagogical. Insider risk is a
   carrier-side institutional issue that must be acknowledged for
   defense to be deployed.

2. **The cascade in stage 7 (Gmail / Apple ID / Sagicor) is the most
   important architectural lesson.** SMS-2FA is a single point of
   failure across the customer's entire digital identity. This is
   the executive-level lesson: migration to authenticator apps or
   FIDO2 passkeys is not a nice-to-have. It is the defense.

3. **Tanya did nothing wrong.** This is a deliberate departure from
   the Phishing (F1081) and Vishing (F1088) animations where the
   victim was tricked into providing something. SIM swap doesn't
   require the victim to do anything. The lesson trainees must
   internalize: not every fraud victim made a mistake. Asking
   "what did the customer do wrong?" in a SIM swap dispute is
   asking the wrong question. The right questions are: when did
   the number port, what device fingerprint authorized the
   transfers, has bank-telco SIM-swap-API integration been queried.
